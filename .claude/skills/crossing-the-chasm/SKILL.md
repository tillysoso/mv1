# Crossing the Chasm

Framework for moving from early adopters to the mainstream market. Most tech companies fail not from bad products, but from applying early-market tactics to the mainstream market.

## Core Principle

"There is a chasm between early adopters and the mainstream market."

Early adopters (Visionaries) and mainstream customers (Pragmatists) want fundamentally different things. What wins over one actively repels the other.

## The Technology Adoption Lifecycle

```
Innovators → Early Adopters → [CHASM] → Early Majority → Late Majority → Laggards
    2.5%          13.5%                       34%             34%           16%
```

**The Chasm**: Between Early Adopters and Early Majority. Most companies die here.

## Visionaries vs. Pragmatists

| Dimension | Visionaries (Early Adopters) | Pragmatists (Early Majority) |
|-----------|------------------------------|------------------------------|
| Goal | Revolutionary change | Evolutionary improvement |
| Reference | Other visionaries | Industry colleagues |
| Risk tolerance | High — expect bugs | Low — want proven solutions |
| Support expectations | Will work through problems | Want complete solutions |
| Decision basis | "This could change everything" | "Who else uses this?" |

## Crossing the Chasm: The D-Day Analogy

Don't attack the whole market at once. Choose a **beachhead** — a specific, small segment — and win it completely.

### Step 1: Select a Beachhead Segment

Criteria for a good beachhead:
- Specific, identifiable group of customers
- Compelling reason to buy (urgent, painful problem)
- Complete product can be delivered today
- Can be dominated (become #1)
- Good reference for adjacent segments

### Step 2: The Bowling Pin Strategy

Win the beachhead → use that as a reference to win adjacent segments.

Each segment you win makes the next easier. The beachhead is the lead pin.

### Step 3: The Whole Product

Pragmatists don't buy products — they buy **complete solutions**. Map everything the customer needs to succeed:

- **Generic product**: What ships in the box
- **Expected product**: What customers assume is included
- **Augmented product**: What differentiates beyond expectations
- **Potential product**: What could be added in the future

For the chasm, you must deliver the Expected product at minimum.

### Step 4: Position Against a Competitor

Pragmatists need to categorize you. Define:

- **Market alternative**: The incumbent they'd use if you didn't exist
- **Product alternative**: A similar startup that validates the category

Position: "Unlike [market alternative], our product [key differentiator] for [beachhead segment]."

## Positioning Template

"For [beachhead customers] who [have this problem], [product name] is a [category] that [key benefit]. Unlike [market alternative], [product] [key differentiator]."

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Fix |
|---------|-----|
| Trying to win all segments at once | Pick one beachhead and dominate it |
| Selling vision to pragmatists | Lead with references, ROI, and proven outcomes |
| No whole product strategy | Map everything needed for customer success |
| Targeting by demographic not problem | Segment by compelling reason to buy |
| Letting early adopters define your roadmap | They're not your mainstream customer |

## Quick Diagnostic

| Question | If No → Action |
|----------|----------------|
| Can you name your specific beachhead segment? | Narrow down — "SMB" is not specific enough |
| Do you have 3+ successful reference customers in it? | Get those wins before expanding |
| Have you mapped the whole product? | List everything pragmatists need to succeed |
| Do pragmatists in this segment have a compelling reason to buy? | Validate with 10 interviews in segment |
| Do you have a market alternative to position against? | Identify the incumbent they use today |
