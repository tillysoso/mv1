# Jobs to Be Done (JTBD)

Framework for understanding what customers truly hire products to do, rather than focusing on features or demographics.

## Core Principle

"Customers don't buy products — they 'hire' them to do a specific job in their lives."

A **job** is the progress a customer wants to make in specific circumstances. It's not a feature, a demographic, or a task list — it's the underlying motivation.

## Job Statement Format

Structure: **When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [expected outcome].**

- Focus on the desired progress, not the solution
- Never mention a specific product or feature
- Include the circumstance that triggers the need

Example: "When I'm feeling overwhelmed before a big meeting, I want to quickly organize my thoughts, so I can present confidently."

## The Four Forces of Progress

What determines whether a customer adopts a new product or sticks with the status quo:

| Force | Direction | Description |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| **Push** (from current) | → Switch | Frustrations with current solution |
| **Pull** (toward new) | → Switch | Attraction to new solution's outcomes |
| **Anxiety** (about new) | ← Stay | Concerns about switching risks |
| **Habit** (of current) | ← Stay | Inertia and comfort with current behavior |

To drive adoption: amplify Push + Pull, reduce Anxiety + Habit.

## Big Hire vs. Little Hire

- **Big Hire**: The purchase decision (customer buys the product)
- **Little Hire**: Each repeated use decision (customer actually uses it)

Many products succeed at Big Hire but fail at Little Hire. Both must be optimized.

## Competitive Landscape (JTBD lens)

JTBD competitors are not just direct alternatives — they're anything the customer might hire for the same job.

Example: Hiring a milkshake for the "make my boring commute less tedious" job — competitors are bananas, bagels, and car radio, not just other milkshakes.

Map competitors by: same job, different solution. This reveals non-obvious threats and opportunities.

## Discovery Interviews

**Timeline-based method** — trace the customer's journey backward from purchase:

1. When did you first think about changing?
2. What happened that day/week that triggered the search?
3. What did you try first? Why didn't that work?
4. When did you decide to switch?
5. What were you hoping would be different?

**Listen for**: passive looking → active looking → decision event → consuming.

## Job-Centered Design

Build around completing the job, not around features:

1. Define the job (not the feature)
2. Map the job's progress steps (before, during, after)
3. Identify friction at each step
4. Design to reduce friction or amplify the pull

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Fix |
|---------|-----|
| "Our users are 25–35 year old professionals" | Define the job, not the demographic |
| "Users want feature X" | What job does feature X help them accomplish? |
| Ignoring indirect competitors | Map all solutions to the same job |
| Building what users say they want | Observe what they hire and fire |

## Quick Diagnostic

| Question | If No → Action |
|----------|----------------|
| Can you state the job without naming a feature? | Reframe: "When [situation], I want [progress]..." |
| Have you mapped all four forces for each segment? | Interview churned + retained users |
| Do you know the first moment of passive looking? | Add timeline questions to discovery interviews |
| Is your competitive set defined by job, not category? | List everything hired for the same job |
